Hear the problem. 

I put that on a sticky note and attached it my monitor. It distills everything below in a single phrase that I can quickly process when the worst of my communication tendencies try to rear their ugly head. I'm not the worst communicator but I think its important to be thoughtful on how you speak with folks especially in the age of remote work and Slack conversations.

Lets back up a little bit, my current employment situation has given me a bit too much time to think about everything. What can I optimize, where am I lacking and need improvement. In an attempt to keep better tabs on the industry I discovered podcasts, like 10 years late but, better late than never. While the original idea was prescriptive I've come to really enjoy them. One of the topics that was particularly interesting, I think because I hadn't ever thought of it intentionally, was communication as a skill. It seems strange writing that out as its a fundamental skill we use everyday and maybe thats why it never garnered my attention. 

Why think about something I've been doing since I was two? 

But the discussions were interesting in that the folks were eloquent and they discussed not only how to communicate an idea but how do you do so to actually get your point made or sway some minds. Not in a manipulative way but setting out your arguements succinctly and with the proper emphasis so that the listener picks up what you're actually trying to convey. 

I wrote down a few things that I think unpack my sticky note into something more concrete.

1. Don't be an asshole
	1. Seems pretty simple right? Well I've certainly been guilty of it and have worked with folks that could have used that on their monitor. A story that comes to mind from back in my DO days. We were having some sort of issue with Juniper, I have no idea what it was now, and I hold my self to high standard and was expecting the same from a vendor we spent a fair amount of money with.  And I was overly direct and pushed back on their responses as not good enough. All of this was via email. And then I actually met those guys I was awful to and something along the lines of "The guy that's been really hard on us" was said. I felt a little abashed now having to face those folks but I didn't really learn a lesson from it. Not only after gaining some maturity and self reflection does it strike me how poorly I was acting. 
2. Listen to people, like actually listen.
	1. I'm also of guilty of hearing someone describe an issue and immediately start internalizing how I'd approach it, what went wrong and why. While troubleshooting things quickly isn't inherently bad but missing out on exactly what their problem is. Don't try to solve the problem you think they have, help them solve the problem they have. 
3. Pull the anxiety out of room by claiming the blame for the problem. (With the caveat that this works better at director level — context matters.)
4. Focus on presenting solutions not problems. Or if presenting a problem, at least have a suggestion on how to move forward.
	1. I've done this a ton. "The framework we're using is awful" I'm paraphrasing and I'd like to imagine I wasn't quite this bad but even if I was it's still not the right way to critique something else has built. I think a fair response to this is, "Ok you build something better". 
5. It's us vs the problem as opposed to you vs me.
	1. I really like this one
6. Ask for help or clarity instead of assuming incompetence.
